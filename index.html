Project Management Foundations

Project: A temporary endeavor undertaken to create a unique product, service, or result. Projects drive change and enable business value creation. They have a definite beginning and end.
Program: A group of related projects, subsidiary programs, and program activities managed in a coordinated way to obtain benefits not available from managing them individually. A project may or may not be part of a program. Focuses on achieving program benefits.
Portfolio: A collection of projects, programs, subsidiary portfolios, and operations managed as a group to achieve strategic objectives. The components of a portfolio may not necessarily be interdependent or directly related. Focuses on maximizing the value of the portfolio and alignment with organizational strategy.

A PMO is an organizational structure that standardizes the project-related governance processes and facilitates the sharing of resources, methodologies, tools, and techniques.

Supportive: Provides a consultative role. Supplies templates, best practices, training, and lessons learned. It's a project repository. Has low control.
Controlling: Provides support and requires compliance through various means. It adopts project management frameworks or methodologies, uses specific templates, forms, and tools, and conforms to governance frameworks. Has moderate control.
Directive: Takes control of the projects by directly managing them. Project managers are assigned by and report to the PMO. Has high control.

Organizations select projects that best align with their strategic goals. This is often done using economic models for project selection, which fall under the category of benefit measurement methods.

  • Net Present Value (NPV): The present value of total benefits (income) minus the costs over many time periods. It accounts for the time value of money. Higher NPV is better.
  • Internal Rate of Return (IRR): The interest rate at which the project inflows (revenues) and project outflows (costs) are equal. It represents the "inherent discount rate" of the project. Higher IRR is better.
  • Payback Period: The length of time it takes for the organization to recover its investment in a project. It does not account for the time value of money or income beyond the payback period. Shorter payback period is generally better.
  • Benefit-Cost Ratio (BCR): Compares the financial benefits to the costs. A ratio > 1 means benefits are greater than costs. Higher BCR is better.
  • Opportunity Cost: The value of the project or opportunity that was not chosen. This is the benefit lost by selecting another alternative.
  • Sunk Costs: Expended costs. Sunk costs should not be considered when deciding whether to continue with a troubled project.

Domain I: People (42% of Exam)

Servant Leader: The primary focus is on understanding and addressing the needs and development of team members to enable the highest possible team performance. This is the preferred style for the PMP exam. A servant leader focuses on removing impediments, facilitating, and coaching.

Emotional Intelligence (EI): The ability to identify, assess, and manage one's own emotions, the emotions of others, and of groups. Key components are:

  • Self-awareness: Understanding your own emotions and how they affect your performance.
  • Self-management: Controlling your emotions and impulses.
  • Social awareness: Perceiving and understanding the emotions of others (empathy).
  • Relationship management: Managing interactions successfully.

Top sources of conflict on projects: 1. Schedules, 2. Project priorities, 3. Resources, 4. Technical opinions.

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs: People are motivated to fulfill a hierarchy of needs. A lower-level need must be satisfied before a higher-level need can emerge as a motivator. (Level 1: Physiological -> 2: Safety -> 3: Social -> 4: Esteem -> 5: Self-Actualization).
Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory:
  • Hygiene Factors: e.g., salary, working conditions, job security. Their absence causes dissatisfaction, but their presence does not necessarily motivate. They are expected.
  • Motivating Agents: e.g., achievement, recognition, responsibility, challenging work, growth. These are the factors that truly motivate people to perform better.
McGregor's Theory X and Y:
  • Theory X Manager: Believes employees are inherently lazy and must be micromanaged and controlled.
  • Theory Y Manager: Believes employees are self-motivated, enjoy their work, and seek responsibility. PMI assumes a Theory Y approach is best.

Domain II: Process (50% of Exam)

The PMP exam framework is built upon 5 Process Groups (the project lifecycle) and 10 Knowledge Areas (the subject areas of project management).

The 5 Process Groups:

  1. Initiating: Defines and authorizes the project or phase. Key outputs: Project Charter, Stakeholder Register.
  2. Planning: Defines objectives and plans the course of action. Key output: Project Management Plan.
  3. Executing: Performs the work defined in the plan. Key outputs: Deliverables, Work Performance Data.
  4. Monitoring & Controlling: Tracks progress, compares it to the plan, and takes corrective action. Key outputs: Change Requests, Work Performance Reports.
  5. Closing: Finalizes all activities to formally close the project or phase. Key outputs: Final Product Transition, Lessons Learned Register.

The 10 Knowledge Areas:

Integration, Scope, Schedule, Cost, Quality, Resources, Communications, Risk, Procurement, and Stakeholder Management.

This is the PM's primary role: pulling all the pieces together.

Project Charter: Formally authorizes the project and gives the PM authority to apply organizational resources. It includes high-level requirements, objectives, assumptions, and constraints.

Project Management Plan: A comprehensive document that defines how the project is executed, monitored, controlled, and closed. It is a collection of all other management plans (scope, schedule, cost, etc.).

Perform Integrated Change Control: The process of reviewing all change requests; approving changes and managing changes to deliverables, organizational process assets, project documents, and the project management plan; and communicating their disposition.

Work Breakdown Structure (WBS): A deliverable-oriented hierarchical decomposition of the work to be executed by the project team. The lowest level is the work package. It represents 100% of the project scope.

Critical Path Method: The sequence of scheduled activities that determines the duration of the project. It is the longest path through the network diagram and has the least amount of float, often zero.

Schedule Compression:

  • Crashing: Adding resources to shorten the schedule duration for the least incremental cost. Always increases costs.
  • Fast Tracking: Performing activities or phases in parallel that would normally be done in sequence. Always increases risk.

Three-Point Estimating (PERT): An estimating technique that uses a weighted average of three estimates: Optimistic (O), Pessimistic (P), and Most Likely (M). Formula: (O + 4M + P) / 6.

EVM integrates scope, schedule, and cost performance. Key terms: PV (Planned Value), EV (Earned Value), AC (Actual Cost), BAC (Budget at Completion).

Domain III: Business Environment (8% of Exam)

Compliance involves conforming to rules, such as specifications, policies, standards, or laws. The project manager is responsible for identifying and adhering to all applicable compliance requirements.

  • Internal Compliance: Adherence to the organization's own policies, procedures, and governance frameworks (e.g., PMO templates, HR policies, quality standards).
  • External Compliance: Adherence to laws, regulations, and standards imposed by government, industry, or other external bodies (e.g., environmental regulations, safety standards, data privacy laws like GDPR).
  • The project manager must confirm compliance requirements, classify them, analyze threats to compliance, and plan the necessary actions and resources to address them. This is part of risk management.

Value is the ultimate indicator of project success. The project manager must ensure the project delivers its intended business value and enables the benefits defined.

  • Business Case: Justifies the project in terms of its value to the business. It links the project to the organization's strategic objectives and is a key input to the Project Charter.
  • Benefits Management Plan: Outlines how and when the project's benefits will be delivered and measured. The PM ensures the project's outputs and outcomes can enable those benefits.
  • This requires continuous monitoring of the project's alignment with business goals and stakeholder needs. If the business case is no longer valid, the PM has a responsibility to recommend project termination.

Projects inherently create change within an organization. The project manager must understand and manage the impact of this change on the organization and its stakeholders to ensure the project's outcome is successfully adopted.

  • Assess the organization's culture and readiness for change: Understand how the organization will react to change.
  • Develop a change management strategy: Work with stakeholders to prepare them for the change. This might involve communication plans, training sessions, and addressing resistance.
  • Ensure a smooth transition: The project's deliverable must be successfully integrated into the organization's ongoing operations to realize its value. This is a key part of the Close Project or Phase process.

Exam Tips & Tricks

The exam is based on PMI's ideal world of project management. Assume the following unless a question states otherwise:

  • A formal project charter is always created and it authorizes the project manager.
  • The project manager was assigned during initiating and participated in creating the charter.
  • A comprehensive project management plan is created and approved before executing begins.
  • Formal, integrated change control is always followed. Changes are not made on the fly.
  • The project manager is proactive and addresses issues before they become problems. Risk management is performed.
  • Lessons learned are documented throughout the project, not just at the end.
  • The project manager has authority and should not escalate problems to the sponsor unless they are outside their control or require resources beyond their authority.
  • The project manager should always act ethically and professionally.
  • Identify the actual question: It's often the last sentence. "What should the project manager do NEXT?" or "What should the project manager have done to prevent this?"
  • Assess the situation: Which process group are you in (Initiating, Planning, Executing, M&C, Closing)? Is it a predictive, agile, or hybrid environment? What is the core problem vs. the symptoms?
  • Think "Process First": Before taking action, what does the process say you should do? Often the answer is to review a plan, analyze the situation, or assess impacts.
  • Don't solve the problem, address it: The initial step is often to investigate, not to implement a solution. For example, if a stakeholder is unhappy, first meet with them to understand their concerns, don't immediately change the project.
  • Look for the "textbook" answer: Refer to the formal processes. If there's a scope issue, the answer might involve the change control process. If there's a conflict, it might involve a specific conflict resolution technique.
  • Eliminate bad choices: Rule out answers where the PM is passive ("wait and see"), escalates prematurely, acts unethically, or takes action without analysis.
